Sapporo Trip

“Kanto-orowa-yaku-saku-no-arankep-shinep-ka-isam” - “Everything in this world has a role to play”

(English translated from Ainu language)

A few weekends ago I had the wonderful opportunity to visit the Japanese prefecture of Hokkaido! Here it is on a map. It is the largest prefecture in Japan known for many things including corn, beer (Sapporo beer), miso ramen, seafood/fishing, a lamb BBQ dish called Genghis Khan, as well as being home of the native Ainu people with their own culture, history and language (more on that later). I hope you enjoy reading about my experience as well as enjoy some of the pictures I took! It was my first time travelling with my new camera (a Sony Alpha 6100) so I was excited to use it.

We arrived in Hokkaido on the 7th! By “we” I mean me and many of the other Miyako ALTs as we had planned this trip together. I remember being shocked by how cold it was, but feeling at home since I'm used to colder temperatures, and it only drops to low 50s in Fereinheit (10C) in Miyako.

On top of being a city absolutely piled in snow (at least when we visited) it is laid out in a grid pattern with roads that tend to be wider than other cities in Japan. Me and my friend Raphael spent a lot of the trip raving about how much Sapporo had felt like Chicago.

To take this chronologically from day 1, we first went to the snow festival! The snow festival is an event in Sapporo at Odori Park where people can see various snow sculptures, enjoy local Hokkaido food and see live performances. According to the internet it started in 1950 when some high school students built six Sculptures in the park. It has since grown to have a local sculpture competition, an international competition as well as various other sculptures created as advertisements for anime and mobile games. Visit the Sapporo Snow Festival website here! You can switch it to English in the bottom right corner of the screen.

Here are some of my favorite sculptures!

these are a few submissions for an international competition the festival hosted. All of the international submissions were made with a small excerpt from the artist about the statue. The winner this year was from Mongolia!

The title of the Mongolian one is “Life”. Here is a description of the piece from the artist: “It’s the nature of the world that a man and a woman are destined to mate and build their livelihoods, support their offspring and cultivate their ancestries. Mongolians have been educating their children about this precious relationship in human life since ancient times. Among them, the bear is represented as a symbol of strength, the wolf is the symbol of endurance, the swan is the symbol of true love. We made this work based on the fairy tale "The swan became a symbol of love", and it is a depiction of a couple of young people sitting facing each other and trying to create their lives. Life is represented by trees. The work depicts a pair of young couples cherishing the tree of life in their hands, covering it with their bodies and protecting it, and double guarding it with idols depicted in their armor. May all the families of the world live peacefully and the tree of life grows and blossoms.”

I think it’s beautiful how the sculpture invokes Mongolian fairy tales, values and symbols while telling a story that many can relate to. Especially in a world where many young people worry about their futures, livelihoods and ability to create a family (myself included), there’s peace and comfort to be found in this sculpture. I especially enjoyed how the sculpture is closed off. Most of the detail is hidden by the sweeping traditional Mongolian clothes worn by the subjects. It gives a warm feeling of protection, and that the struggles and the fruit of this beautiful relationship are no one’s to bear but the two partaking in it.

After the snow festival, we stopped at a cafe and then went to Sapporo’s Ramen Yokocho. For those who don’t know, a yokocho is a narrow alleyway usually filled with shoulder to shoulder bars with lively atmospheres. This vibe is recreated in Sapporo with the concept of having various ramen shops that pack people in tight lines. Here is the ramen that I tried! It was a very tasty shrimp miso ramen. All of the ramen shops looked really good. If I’m back in Sapporo I will certainly visit again to try some of the other varieties!

After finishing the Ramen, I returned back to our sleepy town outside of central Sapporo called Shiroishi. I went with Raphael to a small bar to grab some final drinks, and try yellowfin jaw for the first time, and then went back to the Airbnb to sleep.

The next day we started by picking up some rental cars and getting breakfast. Here’s a picture of the breakfast I had! It was rice, bacon, eggs, salad and a yummy cafe mocha.

After we finished eating we drove up to the Ainu cultural center. Here I’ll take a break to write some things about Japan as a country.

People often say that Japan is a monocultural monoethnic society (including many Japanese and the Japanese government). These people are wrong. As much as the Japanese government would like to portray the idea that all Japanese share an ethnic background, and that the number of immigrants is 0 (the Japanese government gets away with this number by defining an “immigrant” as someone who becomes a citizen when they cross the border), Japan is actually a multi-ethnic and culturally diverse country.

It is hard to find actual numbers since the Japanese government doesn’t recognize certain ethnic backgrounds as distinct ethnic backgrounds. For example, in the country’s census data it lists Ainu people and Ryukyuans as Japanese. While Ryukyuans may have similar ancestry to Japanese, their history is their own, that of the Ryukyu Kingdom, until they were colonized by Japan in the late 1800s. Same as the Ainus, with their own civilizations, language and history. While it is estimated that these groups make up about 1.2% of Japan’s population, it is also estimated that many who identify as Japanese also have mixed Ryukyuan or Ainu blood. By census reports, another 2.2% of the country is not Japanese (Korean, China, and Indian being the largest of these ethnic groups). It is also important to consider that when looking at roughly 3-4% of the entire Japan population, we're talking about up to about 4 million people.

I mention this because I’m going to share everything I found interesting about the Ainu cultural center. It’s important to understand that what I am going to share is not the culture of Japan, but the culture of an oppressed and subjugated ethnic minority in Japan that has fought for its right to practice and share its culture. They are also not the only ethnic minority in Japan experiencing this discrimination. I am grateful that they are open to sharing their culture, as there are many interesting and beautiful clothes, dances, beliefs and a language entirely unique, with no known connected language families.

Here is a picture I got of the front of the center. It was a beautiful snowy day. Most of it was free. The only area requiring an entrance fee was the display room that took a fee of only 300 yen (like 2 USD).

Upon entering I was immediately taken aback by how intricate all of the clothing was. Something that also shocked me was to learn that a lot of their traditional clothing was made of woven tree bark.

Here is an image of a traditional summer outfit (in pink) and a traditional winter outfit (in blue). The summer outfit is made of keri (sandals), sakhos (leggings) and a short sleeved robe-like top. These sakhos and keri are sometimes made from dried grass, and sometimes made from tree bark. The winter outfit has tree bark boots called at-keri, leggings called hoshi (often made from cotton or thread), a thicker outer layer top and a hood called a konchi.

The ingenuity it took to make these clothes is the result of a culture that believes in living in harmony with nature and using all you can of the resources provided by the world. Ainu believed in what they called Kamuy, or their understanding of gods. Kamuy take many shapes and sizes in the natural world, but are thought of to be visitors from another world. For example, bears are often thought of as Kamuy. A bear is a god that visits from another world and provides food, clothing and tools to the Ainu. When they kill a bear, they would use every part of it. If the bear had a cub, they would capture it and raise it on their own, taking good care of it until sending it back to the world of the Kamuy. The thought is that taking care of good Kamuy that brought them resources (thus bringing them life) would leave those Kamuy with positive impressions of people making them more likely to visit the human world again. This belief is realized in a people that have a deep ingenuity and a careful understanding of the balance between themselves and nature, often taking care of their society as well as the world around them.

Here are some images of the inside and outside of a cise, or a traditional Ainu house. The outside is made of thatch and wood. There’s unique furniture on the inside including a fire pit, a baby cradle and various mats. Fires are necessary to cook food and heat up the house. A cise was also often a gathering place to perform various traditions such as dances, storytelling and music.

Storytelling is incredibly important to the Ainu people, since their language had only gained a written standardization recently in the 20th century, using the Latin alphabet as well as another standardization using Japanese Katakana. This means until the 20th century, all language related information about the Ainu had been passed down orally, and all dispersed information about them had either been written by Ainu people who spoke Japanese or by someone who wasn’t Ainu.

I was only there for a short time, but if I revisit Hokkaido I would like to go back or go to Upopoy, which is an Ainu museum and park built to help preserve the culture. It was inspiring to see the dedication and devotion being put into revitalizing and preserving the local culture of the people native to the land in Hokkaido. If you want to learn more here is a link to the website for The Foundation for Ainu Culture

It also reminded me that I also currently live on land colonized by Japan, with a terrible history of subjugation and attempted cultural erasure. I’m learning Sanshin (traditional Okinawan three stringed instrument), which is fun, but it got me wondering what more I could do to engage with the local culture, history and language of Miyakojima.

I always think there is so much to learn from those who are different from us, and it’s incredibly important to preserve various ways of life, language and ways of thinking. That is why the title of this post is a quote that seems to be at the heart of a lot of Ainu beliefs and cultural practices, “Everything in this world has a role to play”.

In other words, everything has inherent meaning in the role it plays in nature. Every part of every animal has its use, and if it is not useful to you it can be useful to the soil, or other animals. Every mountain, every river, every person serves a purpose to the greater ecosystem of this world and the universe. In this realization the Ainu people have lived peacefully, and in harmony with nature. I think this is so far from the way we live life, with so much garbage and waste, so much consumed and forgotten about. But we can learn from their resourcefulness and way of living with nature.

After the Ainu Cultural Center we drove to Hohei Onsen. There we relaxed, and for the first time I got to experience an Onsen while it was snowing! Honestly, the water was probably the coldest (it was still warm but the coldest) onsen water I had been in, but to be outside in the cold weather in hot water was so relaxing. We also enjoyed the views of the snowy mountains!

Afterwards, we went to the restaurant attached to the onsen. There we ate the famous Hokkaido dish Genghis Khan (named after one famous Mongolian ruler). It was believed in pre-warring period Japan that Mongolians ate a lot of lamb, and the grill it’s cooked on looks like a traditional Mongolian helmet, thus it was named Genghis Khan. It is barbecue lamb often eaten with vegetables and rice.

The plans for the next day were to go to the Buddha Statue near Sapporo and eat conveyor belt sushi!

First we went to the Buddha statue. The statue is called “Hill of The Buddha” in English and 頭大仏 “Head Buddha Statue” in Japanese. Although some might have the impression that Buddha Statues are a historical remnant of Buddhism in Japan, this one was actually constructed in 2015, demonstrating how Buddhism is actually still very much alive in the country.

The Statue was designed by architect Tadao Ando. His vision for the project was to build a serene and peaceful buddha statue hidden from those outside that would slowly reveal itself to you as you walked closer. Walking around the hill the statue is encased in, the top of the buddha’s head can be seen (thus the name Head Buddha Statue in Japanese). I went in the winter, so the hill had a beautiful coating of fresh white snow. In the summer the snow is replaced with lavender fields.

Once you walk around the hill and arrive at the entrance of the structure, this is what you see. We had seen on Google maps before arriving that the statue closes at 3, and we arrived at around 3:45 (it takes about an hour and half to get there from Sapporo station). However, I made eye contact with a man at the ticket machine, and he gave a big “OK” sign with his hands, so we got to go in! We bought the tickets (300 yen each) and began to approach the entrance of the statue.

Walking down the hall up to the statue kind of feels like boarding a spaceship in some sci-fi movie. As you approach the buddha slowly reveals itself to eventually look like this.

Standing (or sitting) at 13.5 meters tall, the sharp features contrasted with the round face create a warm and peaceful expression on quite an impressive statue.

A trip to this statue certainly isn’t a necessary part of a Sapporo visit, but I saw it in a youtube video by Abroad In Japan and it has stuck in my head since then. For me personally a trip to Sapporo wouldn’t have been complete without a trip to The Hill of The Buddha.

After seeing the statue and making the trip back to Sapporo station, we met up with some more friends and made our way to eat conveyor belt sushi. While conveyor belt sushi is not special to the Hokkaido region, in Miyakojima there is no conveyor belt sushi so we were all excited to go. Seafood is also known as being particularly fresh in Hokkaido to the point where it’s said that even at normal sushi places the fish tastes better. While I’m not a fish expert, some beer and some sushi was a fantastic finish to a day filled with travel and walking.

I hope you enjoyed reading about my trip to Hokkaido! While it wasn’t particularly about Miyako, I hope you were able to learn a bit more about Japan and the world.
